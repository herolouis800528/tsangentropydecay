Einstein’s general relativity teaches us that time and space are not absolute. They bend and shift depending on motion, mass, and perspective. In a universe of relative frames, only one thing remains constant: the speed of light.

But if light is the one universal constant, approximately 300,000 kilometers per second, then why do we still measure it using meters and seconds — both of which are human inventions? Doesn't this choice contradict the deeper philosophical implications of relativity itself?

To explore this, we must revisit the origins of these units.
Time, as we measure it, was derived from the Earth's rotation — long before we understood that Earth orbits the Sun. We once believed the Sun "rose" and "set."
Distance evolved from physical standards — like rods of metal — calibrated by the dimensions of human bodies or landmarks. Yet under varying conditions, these rods contract, expand, and deform. How "universal" is a unit that bends with its environment?

The history of physics is full of elegant frameworks:
Newton gave us gravity.
Maxwell gave us the electromagnetic field.
Einstein gave us relativity.
But as physics shifted from the macroscopic to the microscopic, our models became increasingly abstract. We now describe matter in terms of invisible entities: atoms, nuclei, electrons, quarks, ions, bosons. Many of these have never been directly observed — only inferred through experimental signatures.

And still, we rely on mathematics to give us confidence.
In school, we’re taught that experiments are “successful” if the data forms a clean line or a tidy curve. Deviation is called "noise." A perfect fit is taken as truth.

But what if this elegance conceals a deeper limitation?

If light is the constant, and our theories are built atop it, have we truly explained reality? Or have we merely described it — from the perspective of beings moving unimaginably slowly compared to light itself?

Unlike the Standard Model, the Entropy-Decay Theory does not describe matter in terms of flowing, twisting, or colliding particles. This reorients our perspective away from the behavior of individual atoms and toward the system as a whole.

In classical thermodynamics, entropy is treated as disorder — a chaotic byproduct, often labeled as “noise.” In this framework, however, entropy is not a consequence, but the origin of all observed phenomena. Thus, in the Entropy-Decay Theory, we are not seeking relationships between space and time. Instead, we study the flow of entropy between atoms, across systems, and between celestial bodies. We do not ask what happened when t = 0, but what marked the first step of entropy increase — when ꚍ = 1.
